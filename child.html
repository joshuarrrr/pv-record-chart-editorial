30 Years of Record-Breaking PV Technology

A successful photovoltaic technology must satisfy a few key ingredients: it must efficiently convert sunlight to electricity; it must be economical to manufacture and maintain; and it must be durable enough to have a productive lifetime.

While solar technology has improved greatly in all three aspects over the past few decades, there’s still much room for improvement before photovoltaics start outcompeting traditional fossil-fuel-based energy production. Because manufacturing technology lags laboratory discoveries by many years, most of the photovoltaic cells produced today are based on relatively early generations of PV technology. But researchers in labs, universities, and private companies continue to explore new types of cells and push the capabilities of existing ones. The solar cell of the future may eventually develop from one of these research technologies.

With that in mind, Keith Emery, a senior IEEE member at NREL, has spent decades tracking improvements in solar cell efficiency records, which act as a proxy for tracking progress with different semiconductor technologies since 1976. He’s shared his decades of data with IEEE Spectrum, and we’ve endeavored to make them easier to explore.
